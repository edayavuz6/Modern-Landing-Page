Socrates

"There is only one good, which is knowledge, and only one evil, which is ignorance."

Who is Socrates?

(c. 470 – 399 BC)

The founding father of Western philosophy. He wrote nothing but changed everything through the power of questioning. Known for the Socratic Method, he believed that wisdom begins with acknowledging one's own ignorance and that an unexamined life is not worth living.

Plato

"The beginning is the most important part of the work."

Who is Plato?

(c. 427 – 347 BC)

A student of Socrates and the founder of the Academy, the first institution of higher learning in the Western world. Through his Theory of Forms, he argued that reality exists beyond the physical world, leaving a profound legacy in politics, ethics, and metaphysics.

Aristotle

"Knowing yourself is the beginning of all wisdom."

Who is Aristotle?

(384 – 322 BC)

A student of Plato and the tutor to Alexander the Great. He is considered the father of logic, biology, and zoology. By emphasizing observation and experience, he laid the foundations for the modern scientific method and empirical research.

René Descartes

"I think, therefore I am."

Who is René Descartes?

(1596 – 1650 AD)

Known as the father of modern philosophy and a brilliant mathematician. He sought to build a foundation for all human knowledge through radical doubt. By asserting that the very act of thinking proves one's existence, he bridged the gap between the mind and the physical world.

Immanuel Kant

"Dare to know! Have courage to use your own reason."

Who is Immanuel Kant?

(1724 – 1804 AD)

One of the most influential figures in modern philosophy. He synthesized rationalism and empiricism, arguing that the human mind actively shapes our perception of the world. His "Categorical Imperative" remains a cornerstone of modern ethical thought.

Friedrich Nietzsche

"He who has a why to live for can bear almost any how."

Who is Friedrich Nietzsche?

(1844 – 1900 AD)

A cultural critic and philosopher known for his radical questioning of objective truth and morality. He introduced the concept of the "Übermensch" (Overman), encouraging individuals to transcend traditional values and create their own meaning in life.
